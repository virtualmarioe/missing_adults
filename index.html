Abstract

Person missingness is an enigmatic and frequent phenomenon that can bring about negative consequences for the missing person, their family, and society in general. Age-related cognitive changes and a higher vulnerability to dementia can increase the propensity of older adults to go missing. Thus, it is necessary to better understand the phenomenon of missingness in older adults. The present study sought to identify individual and environmental factors that might predict whether an older adult reported missing will be found. Supervised machine learning models were used based on the missing person cases open data of Colombia between 1930 and June 2021 (n = 7855). Classification algorithms were trained to predict whether an older adult who went missing would eventually be found. The classification models with the best performance in the test data were those based on gradient boosting. Particularly, the Gradient Boosting Classifier and the Light Gradient Boosting Machine algorithms showed, respectively, 10% and 9% greater area under the curve (AUC) of the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve than a data-driven, reference model based on the mean of the reported time elapsed since the missingness observed in the training data. The features with the greatest contribution to the classification were the time since the missingness, the place where it occurred, and the age and sex of the missing person. The present results shed light on the societal phenomenon of person missingness while setting the ground for the application of machine learning models in cases of missing older persons.

Methods

Open data from the Sistema de Información Red de Desaparecidos y Cadáveres (SIRDEC) of the Colombian National Institute of Legal Medicine were processed in a three-phase pipeline using PyCaret 2.3.6 and scikit-learn 1.0.2.

1
Data cleaning
Exclude null Age/Date, forced disappearance, < 60 y, “Found dead” and cases outside Colombia.
2
Feature preparation
Standard scaling of numeric features, one-hot encoding of categorical variables, label encoding of the target.
3
Class balancing
SMOTE oversampling and random undersampling applied to the training set only.
4
Model training
10-fold stratified CV across a broad set of classifiers; top models selected by accuracy and AUC.
5
Interpretation
SHAP values extracted from the GBC to rank and interpret feature contributions.

Predictor variables

Environmental (extrinsic)

Date of missingness — days elapsed since the report until 30 July 2021.

Place of missingness — municipality population size in Colombia.

Individual (intrinsic)

Age at the time of missingness (all examples ≥ 60 years).

Sex, marital status, education level, and vulnerability factor.

Target

Found vs. Still missing (binary classification). Only alive outcomes retained to focus on findability.

Takeaways

+10%
AUC gain

The Gradient Boosting Classifier increases ROC–AUC by 10 percentage points over a data-informed baseline that uses only the mean elapsed time.

83%
Recall on “Found”

Using random undersampling of the dominant “Still missing” class on the training set raises recall on the minority “Found” class from 52% to 83%.

4
Core predictors

Time since missingness, municipality size, sex, and age of the missing person concentrate most of the predictive signal in the model.

Robust
Sensitivity analysis

Restricting the sample to cases from 2000 onwards preserves the model’s performance and feature ranking, confirming robustness to historical-technological change.

Understanding ROC-AUC

AUC (Area Under the ROC Curve) is the metric the authors optimize. It answers a simple question: if we draw one “found” case and one “still missing” case at random, how often does the model assign a higher score to the found case? A perfect model scores 1.0; random guessing scores 0.5. Drag the sliders below to see how the three most-confused factors — separation, threshold and class balance — each influence what you see.

1. Separation moves AUC

The only thing that changes AUC is how much the “found” and “still-missing” score distributions overlap. More overlap → noisier model → lower AUC.

2. Threshold picks a point

The decision threshold slides your operating point along the ROC curve (trading recall for false-alarms). It never changes the underlying AUC.

3. Prevalence hides in precision

AUC, TPR and FPR are invariant to class balance, but precision reacts sharply. That’s exactly why the paper undersamples the majority class to boost recall from 52% to 83%.
